Complete Door Repair Guide

Doors provide security, privacy, weather protection, and convenient access throughout a property. Over time, normal use, moisture, building movement, worn hardware, and accidental damage can prevent a door from operating correctly. This guide explains common door problems, possible causes, preventative maintenance, and when professional repair may be appropriate.

Common Door Problems

Sticking Doors

A door may rub against the frame or floor because of humidity, loose hinges, swelling wood, paint buildup, or movement within the building.

Door Alignment Problems

Misalignment can prevent the latch from entering the strike plate correctly and may cause the door to swing open or closed by itself.

Loose Hinges

Loose hinge screws can cause sagging, rubbing, uneven gaps, and difficulty opening or closing the door.

Damaged Door Jambs

Door jambs may split, crack, loosen, or rot because of force, moisture, repeated impacts, or failed hardware.

Broken Handles and Locks

Worn or damaged handles, latches, deadbolts, and locksets can affect convenience and property security.

Drafts Around Exterior Doors

Damaged weatherstripping, worn thresholds, and poor alignment can allow outside air, moisture, dust, and insects to enter.

Sliding Door Problems

Sliding doors may become difficult to move because of dirty tracks, worn rollers, damaged guides, or improper alignment.

Door Surface Damage

Scratches, dents, holes, peeling paint, water stains, and damaged veneer can reduce the appearance and durability of a door.

What Causes Doors to Stop Working Correctly?

  • Loose or damaged hinges
  • Changes in humidity
  • Wood expansion or swelling
  • Building settlement
  • Worn rollers or tracks
  • Repeated slamming
  • Water intrusion
  • Damaged frames or jambs
  • Improper installation
  • Paint buildup around the edges
  • Worn latches, handles, or locks
  • Loose or damaged strike plates

How to Inspect a Problem Door

  1. Open and close the door slowly. Notice where the door rubs, sticks, shifts, or makes unusual sounds.
  2. Inspect the gaps around the door. Uneven gaps can indicate sagging hinges, frame movement, or improper alignment.
  3. Check the hinges and screws. Look for loose screws, bent hinge leaves, missing fasteners, or damaged wood.
  4. Test the latch and lock. Confirm whether the latch enters the strike plate smoothly and whether the lock operates without excessive force.
  5. Inspect the frame and jamb. Look for cracks, splitting, moisture damage, loose trim, and signs of previous forced entry.
  6. Check exterior weather seals. Inspect the weatherstripping, door sweep, and threshold for gaps, wear, or damage.

Simple Door Maintenance

Routine maintenance can prevent many common door problems and extend the life of the door, frame, and hardware.

  • Tighten loose hinge screws
  • Clean sliding door tracks
  • Lubricate hinges and moving hardware when appropriate
  • Replace worn weatherstripping
  • Inspect exterior doors for moisture damage
  • Repair peeling paint or failed sealant
  • Avoid repeatedly slamming doors
  • Keep thresholds and tracks free of dirt
  • Address small frame cracks before they spread
  • Test locks and latches regularly

Sticking Door Repair

A sticking door should first be inspected to determine exactly where contact occurs. Common contact points include the upper latch-side corner, the lower edge, and the hinge-side edge.

Loose hinges should be corrected before removing material from the door. If the door has swollen because of moisture, the underlying moisture source should also be investigated. Trimming or sanding a door before identifying the cause can create excessive gaps later.

Door Jamb and Frame Damage

A damaged jamb can affect both door operation and security. Minor cracks may sometimes be reinforced, while severely split, rotted, or missing sections may require partial or complete jamb replacement.

Exterior door frame damage should be addressed promptly because it may allow water intrusion and reduce the strength of the lock and strike plate area.

Exterior Door Weather Protection

Exterior doors should create a consistent seal around the top, sides, and bottom. Light, drafts, moisture, or insects entering around the door can indicate failed weatherstripping, a worn sweep, threshold problems, or poor alignment.

Maintaining the paint, sealant, weatherstripping, and threshold helps protect the door and surrounding frame from moisture damage.

Sliding Door Maintenance

Sliding patio and closet doors depend on clean tracks, functional rollers, and proper alignment. Dirt and debris should be removed before adjusting or replacing hardware.

If a sliding door continues to bind, lift, derail, or scrape after cleaning, the rollers, guides, track, or frame may require repair.

When DIY Door Repair May Work

Homeowners may be able to handle minor maintenance such as tightening accessible screws, cleaning tracks, replacing simple weatherstripping, lubricating compatible hardware, or touching up minor surface damage.

Repairs involving structural damage, security hardware, rotted wood, exterior water intrusion, glass, fire-rated doors, or major alignment problems may require professional assistance.

When to Call a Professional

  • The door will not close or lock securely
  • The jamb or frame is split
  • The door has significant water or rot damage
  • The hinges have pulled out of the frame
  • The door continues to sag after tightening hardware
  • The exterior door allows major drafts or moisture intrusion
  • The sliding door repeatedly comes off its track
  • The door or frame was damaged during forced entry
  • The repair involves a fire-rated door
  • The opening requires reframing or extensive carpentry

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does my door suddenly stick?

Humidity, loose hinges, wood swelling, paint buildup, or building movement can cause a door to begin sticking unexpectedly.

Can a damaged door jamb be repaired?

Many jambs can be repaired, but the correct method depends on the location and severity of the damage. Severely split, rotted, or missing sections may require replacement.

Why will my door latch not line up?

The door may be sagging, the hinges may be loose, the strike plate may have moved, or the frame may have shifted.

Should I replace or repair my door?

Repair may be practical when the door is structurally sound and the damage is limited. Replacement may be more appropriate when the door is badly warped, rotted, cracked, insecure, or extensively damaged.

How can I reduce drafts around an exterior door?

Inspect the weatherstripping, door sweep, threshold, frame sealant, and door alignment. Worn or damaged materials should be replaced after the source of the gap is identified.
